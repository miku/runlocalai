# Demo Use Cases — Run Local AI Workshop

Each demo is designed to be runnable live in Jan AI with a mix of local and remote models.
No special setup beyond what is installed at the workshop.
Each task targets one or more audience segments.

---

## Librarian use cases

### 1. Catalog description refinement
> You have a book description that reads like a library catalog entry — dry, sparse, 3 lines.
> Ask the model to expand it into a 150-word blurb suitable for a library website,
> keeping all factual details intact while making it readable for a general audience.

*Audience: Public services / cataloging librarians*
*What it shows: writing quality varies dramatically with model size; the same prompt on a
tiny local model vs. a cloud model produces very different blurb quality*

### 2. Subject heading suggestion
> Paste a paragraph from a manuscript or article abstract.
> Ask: "What subject headings (from a standard classification like DDC or LOBOK) would
> you assign this text? Output as a JSON object with fields: main_heading, secondary_headings, confidence (0-1), reasoning."

*Audience: Cataloging / metadata staff*
*What it shows: structured output works reliably only with capable models; smaller models
produce messy JSON that breaks downstream processing*

### 3. OPAC search query transformation
> Paste a user's natural-language reference request (e.g. "I need books about how to grow
> tomatoes in small apartments in German cities").
> Ask the model to transform it into 3 structured search queries: one keyword string, one
> title/author pattern, one subject heading combination.

*Audience: Reference desk staff*
*What it shows: even small models are useful here — the task is formatting, not reasoning,*
*and the result is a genuine time-saver*

## Undergraduate students (mixed disciplines)

### 4. Lecture notes summarizer
> Paste 3-4 pages of lecture notes (the longer, fragmented, and more "student-like" the
> better). Ask the model to produce a structured summary with: key concepts, definitions,
> and 3 study questions.

*Audience: All undergrads*
*What it shows: you can paste anything into Jan — no special tool needed; try temperature
0.3 for consistent output*

### 5. Citation formatting
> Paste 5 book/article references in inconsistent formats (some missing authors, some with
> wrong punctuation). Ask the model to normalize all of them into a single citation style
> (e.g. APA 7th).

*Audience: All undergrads doing written work*
*What it shows: model size matters here — small models mix up years and volumes; larger
models are reliable*

### 6. Study partner simulation
> Paste a paragraph from your course text. Ask: "Pretend you are a slightly smarter
> classmate. Point out the one thing in this paragraph that is easiest to misunderstand
> and explain it more clearly."

*Audience: Students of any discipline*
*What it shows: a small model can do this reasonably well — it's about style, not depth*

## Graduate students (Master's / qualifying exams)

### 7. Literature matrix builder
> Paste 6-8 paper abstracts (they can come from a Zotero export, a PDF dump, or just a
> Google Scholar search). Ask the model to build a comparison table with columns:
> research question, methodology, sample/data, key finding, limitation.

*Audience: All graduate students*
*What it shows: this is exactly where model size and context window matter; try the same
task on jan-nano (fails badly) vs. a 70B+ model working correctly*

### 8. Thesis paragraph critique
> Paste a draft paragraph or two from your thesis. Ask the model to act as a critical
> thesis advisor and identify: logical gaps, weak arguments, and sentences that could be
> cut. Output only the critiques, no praise.

*Audience: Master's students writing theirses*
*What it shows: system prompt quality matters as much as model size here*

## PhD students / researchers

### 9. Methodology comparison
> Paste two methodology sections from different papers in your field.
> Ask: "Compare these two approaches along the dimensions: data collection, sampling
> strategy, analytical technique, strengths cited, weaknesses acknowledged, and what each
> would miss. Output as a markdown table."

*Audience: PhD students comparing approaches for their own work*
*What it shows: this is a genuinely useful task — the model doesn't invent anything, it
*reorganizes what's in context, which is exactly what humans do too*

### 10. Cover letter / grant summary drafting
> Paste your project abstract and 3 bullet points about your background.
> Ask the model to draft a 300-word project summary suitable for a funding application,
> followed by a separate 150-word version for a lay audience.

*Audience: PhD students applying for positions, fellowships, or conferences*
*What it shows: writing output quality is excellent even on mid-sized models*

### 11. Reviewer response helper
> Paste reviewer comments from a journal submission.
> Ask the model to: "For each comment, draft a polite, point-by-point response that:
> (1) acknowledges the concern, (2) states what you changed (or why you didn't),
> (3) gives the specific location in the manuscript. Keep tone respectful and professional."

*Audience: PhD students and postdocs with submitted work*
*What it shows: even imperfect drafting saves hours of rewriting; the human still edits*

## Cross-cutting demos (all audience segments)

### 12. The "same prompt, different hardware" demo
> Prompt: "Summarize this paragraph in 3 bullet points, then translate it into German.
> Keep the translation academically register but not overly formal."

Run the same prompt on:
1. **Jan's default 4B model** (local, fast, low quality)
2. **A mid-size model on your Strix Halo** (e.g. Qwen2.5:72B — local but slow to load)
3. **A cloud model via OpenRouter or GWDG proxy** (instant, high quality)

*Everyone sees the spectrum. This is the centerpiece demo.*

### 13. Vision — diagram / chart reading
> Upload a small figure or chart (e.g. a bar chart from a paper, a flow diagram, a phase
> diagram). Ask:
> "Describe what this chart shows. What are the axes? What trends are visible? What
> would you conclude from this data?"

*Audience: all — anyone who reads figures in papers*
*What it shows: Jan supports multimodal input; useful for anyone drowning in PDFs*

### 14. The "don't use AI for this" demo
> Ask a model with insufficient context window to summarize a text that exceeds its limit.
> Or ask a small model to do multi-step math or logic (e.g. "A train leaves Station A at
> 80 km/h. Another leaves Station B at 120 km/h toward Station A. The stations are 500 km
> apart. When do they meet? Show your work.").

*Everyone sees failure modes — this is as important as seeing successes.*

## Assistants demo (techniques)

### 15. Build a saved assistant
> In Jan, create an Assistant:
> - Name: "Academic Editor"
> - System prompt: "You are a strict academic editor. When I paste text, return only:
>   (1) corrected version, (2) list of changes made, (3) readability score 0-10.
>   Do not add praise. Do not add commentary. Be surgical."
> Use it on the same text as Demo 4 vs. the default model, without a system prompt.
> Save time: no need to type the prompt each time.

*Audience: all, but especially librarians and PhD students who write constantly*
*What it shows: system prompts + saved assistants = immediate productivity gain*

## Subject librarian / open science use cases

### 16. Datenbank-Empfehlungstraining (database recommendation training)
> Paste a researcher's description of a project (e.g., "I'm studying the effect of
> microplastics on freshwater organisms in Saxony").
> Ask the model to recommend: (a) 3 databases that would be most relevant, (b) which keywords
> to try in each, (c) which databases are less relevant and why (false-positive risk).

*Audience: Subject librarians who routinely do reference consultations (Fachberatung),
especially for Medizin, Chemie/Physik/Geowissenschaften, Lebenswissenschaften*
*What it shows: even a small model can structure a recommendation task if you give it the
*database list as context. Demonstrate by pasting Web of Science, PubMed, Cochrane, JCR
*descriptions into the prompt as reference material, then asking "which of these should this
*researcher use?"

### 17. Open Science compliance summary
> Paste a German DFG-funded project description or a manuscript methods section.
> Ask: "What are the likely Open Science / funding compliance requirements for this type
> of work? List: data management plan expectations, publication mandate (Saxon OA), code
> sharing expectations, citation requirements. Give confidence levels for each."

*Audience: Librarians who advise on open science policy, PhD students writing grant proposals*
*What it shows: the model doesn't replace a librarian here, but it produces a
*first-pass checklist in seconds — the librarian still validates and fills gaps*

### 18. Journal selection helper
> Paste a manuscript abstract and a short methods summary.
> Ask: "Suggest 5 journals where this paper could be published. For each: journal name,
> typical impact factor range, OA option (hybrid/Gold/hybrid), expected acceptance probability
> (low/med/high), and one reason why it's a fit (or why it might not be)."

*Audience: PhD students and postdocs at "Wissenschaftliches Publizieren – Zeitschriften
*auswählen und bewerten" level*
*What it shows: this is partially hallucination-prone. Use a large model if you want
*actionable suggestions; use a small model to show the limits*

### 19. Scite.ai workflow — "what if we didn't have the license?"
> Paste a paragraph from a paper that makes a specific claim.
> With the GWDG/GWDG proxy running: ask the model to "identify the key factual claim
> in this paragraph and list the types of evidence you'd need to verify it."
> Then show what scite_ai does with the same paragraph: find supporting/contrasting citations.

*Audience: Everyone who reads scientific papers*
*What it shows: local AI can do step 1 (identify claims to verify) but scite/Scite.ai is
*what does step 2 (finding the actual evidence). This frames local AI as complementary to
*licensed tools*

## Medical / life sciences students (specifically for UBL context)

### 20. AMBOSS-style concept explanation
> Paste a medical concept or disease description (e.g., "Pathophysiologie der diabetischen
> Nephropathie").
> Ask: "Erkläre dieses Konzept so, dass ein Medizinstudent im 2. Fachsemester es verstehen
> könnte. Gliedere in: (1) Grundmechanismus, (2) klinische Manifestation, (3) wichtige
> Differenzialdiagnose, (4) häufige Prüfungsfalle. Antworte auf Deutsch."

*Audience: Medizin- und Zahnmedizinstudenten (Vorklinik & Klinik)*
*What it shows: medical content with a moderate model works surprisingly well for
*didactic explanation — but emphasize: never trust medical AI output for clinical decisions*

### 21. PubMed search strategy builder
> Paste a clinical question in PICO format (e.g., "P: Erwachsene mit Typ-2-Diabetes,
> I: SGLT2-Hemmer, C: Standardtherapie, O: kardiovaskuläre Endpunkte").
> Ask: "Build a PubMed search strategy. Include: MeSH terms, free-text terms with
> wildcards, AND/OR/NOT logic, and a note on which filters to apply. Output as a
> copy-paste string and a step-by-step explanation."

*Audience: Students attending "Literaturverwaltung mit Zotero" or Literaturrecherche
*workshops*
*What it shows: this task is genuinely useful. Compare doing it manually (15 min) vs.
*model output (30 sec), then verify with PubMed*

### 22. Cochrane-style systematic review pre-screening
> Paste 15 abstracts from a PubMed search on a clinical topic.
> Ask: "Which of these meet these inclusion criteria? P: patients > 65 years; I: exercise
> training; C: control group; O: functional outcome measures. For each paper, output:
> INCLUDE/EXCLUDE/UNCERTAIN and give a one-sentence reason."

*Audience: PhD students doing systematic reviews, medical residents*
*What it shows: model-assisted screening is real — but the UNCERTAIN category is usually
*the most valuable part, and those are the ones that need human attention*

### 23. Statistik-Konzept-Erklärung (statistics concept explanation)
> Paste a statistics method name and a one-line description of the data (e.g., "t-Test,
> 2 Gruppen, metrische Variable, n=25 pro Gruppe").
> Ask: "Wann wäre dieser Test angemessen? Nenne (1) Voraussetzungen (Assumptions),
> (2) was der p-Wert wirklich bedeutet, (3) ein häufiges Missverständnis, (4) Alternativen,
> wenn die Voraussetzungen nicht erfüllt sind. Antworte auf Deutsch."

*Audience: Naturwissenschaftler*innen (DataViz für Naturwissenschaftler*innen audience)
*What it shows: even for teaching context, a local model can be a useful explainer when
*staff are doing "KI-Lab" workshops*

## Data & visualization / LaTeX (UBL-specific)

### 24. DataViz: chart-type recommendation
> Paste a short description of data (e.g., "Ich habe Messwerte von 30 Probanden über
> 6 Zeitpunkte, gruppiert nach Behandlungsgruppe A und B, mit einer metrischen
> abhängigen Variable").
> Ask: "Welches Diagramm-Typ wäre am geeignetsten für die Visualisierung dieser Daten?
> Begründe kurz, was das Diagramm zeigen kann und was nicht. Nenne auch eine Alternative."

*Audience: DataViz für Naturwissenschaftler*innen workshop participants*
*What it shows: the model understands chart semantics even without seeing the data — it's
*a brainstorming partner for data visualization decisions*

### 25. LaTeX snippet generator
> Paste a description of what you want to typeset (e.g., "Ich brauche eine Tabelle mit
> Überschriften 'Parameter', 'Symbol', 'Einheit', 'Wert' und einer Fußzeile mit
> Signifikanzhinweisen").
> Ask: "Erzeuge den LaTeX-Code für diese Tabelle im dokumentclass article, mit tabularx
> und booktabs, inkl. \caption und \label. Verwende deutschen Text in der Tabelle."

*Audience: "Wissenschaftliche Texte verfassen mit LaTeX" workshop participants*
*What it shows: code generation works well with local models — even the small one gives
*usable LaTeX that mostly works. Mid-size models produce more polished output*

### 26. Scientific poster outline
> Paste an abstract and 2-3 key figures/diagram descriptions.
> Ask: "Entwurf einen wissenschaftlichen Poster-Layout in Textform. Gliedere in:
> (1) Titel & Autorenschaft, (2) Einleitung / Hintergrund, (3) Methoden, (4) Ergebnisse
> — was kommt in jedes Feld, (5) Diskussion, (6) Fazit, (7) Literatur. Schlage die
> optimale Bild-Text-Verteilung vor und die Reihenfolge des Leseflusses."

*Audience: "Wissenschaftliches Poster gestalten" workshop participants*
*What it shows: a practical use that benefits from a local model working on text that
*doesn't leave the room — poster content is often in-progress work*

## Open science / "researcher-as-librarian" (UBL-specific)

### 27. DMP (Datenmanagementplan) draft assistant
> Paste a project description with: research question, data type (e.g., interview
> transcripts, sensor data, survey responses), planned analysis methods, expected outputs.
> Ask: "Entwurfe einen DMP-Abschnitt zu (1) Datenbeschreibung, (2) Speicherung (Wo?
> Wie lange? Wer hat Zugriff?), (3) Dokumentation (welche Metadaten?), (4) Langzeitarchivierung,
> (5) Open Access Publikation. Orientiere an FAIR-Prinzipien. Antworte auf Deutsch."

*Audience: Subject librarians advising on DFG compliance PhD students writing grant proposals*
*What it shows: this is a genuinely high-value task — the model produces a complete
*first draft that a librarian or researcher then edits and validates*

### 28. Preprint vs. peer-reviewed distinction explainer
> Paste a paragraph with a mix of preprint and peer-reviewed claims.
> Ask: "Mark each claim as 'likely peer-reviewed' or 'likely from a preprint based on
> language cues'. Then explain the epistemological difference: what should we treat
> differently because it hasn't been peer-reviewed?"

*Audience: Everyone who reads literature — the "KI-Lab" audience*
*What it shows: teaches source literacy, not just AI literacy*

### 29. E-Book chapter summary (Thieme / Springer)
> Paste a copyright-cleared excerpt from a Thieme or Springer E-Book chapter (or use a
> workshop-legal short excerpt).
> Ask: "Fasse diesen Abschnitt zusammen. Extrahiere: Kernbotschaft (1 Satz), 3 wichtige
> Fakten, 1 klinische/praktische Implikation, 2 Begrifflichkeiten die erklärt werden
> müssen."

*Audience: Medical students, anyone using E-Book-Angebote*
*What it shows: local models can work on licensed content without sending it externally —
*this is the privacy advantage*

### 30. Workshop content generator (for subject librarians)
> Ask: "Entwirfe einen 90-minütigen Workshop für Wissenschaftler*innen zum Thema
> 'KI-Unterstützung bei der Literaturrecherche'. Gliedere in: (a) 15 Min. Einführung
> (Begriffe: Prompt, System-Prompt, Modellgröße), (b) 30 Min. Live-Demo mit konkreten
> Aufgabenstellungen, (c) 30 Min. freie Arbeitsphase mit Aufgabenzetteln, (d) 15 Min.
> Abschluss & Ressourcen. Nenne konkrete Software-Empfehlungen (kostenlos & lizenziert)."

*Audience: Subject librarians who plan their own "KI-Lab" and "KI-Stammtisch" events
*What it shows: this is for the librarians themselves. A subject librarian who is a
*researcher-turned-librarian can use this to bootstrap workshop content*
